# Chapter 7: Climate Finance, RWA, and New Paths to Fair Governance

When we discuss the institutional implementation of climate governance, we are essentially discussing the organization, allocation, and trust surrounding resources.

Climate finance is not merely a technical tool—it is a question of trust mechanisms; not just about capital flows, but about civilizational structures.

Behind this challenge lies not only asset classification logic, but deeper questions: **Who defines value? Who controls the language of asset verification? Who designs the risk assessment models?**

At the heart of all these is the construction of institutional trust.

## 7.1 Trust Disparities in the Global Financial System

Mainstream green finance today is built on a **mathematical-trust model**:

- Centered around contracts, algorithms, and ratings
- Generates institutional credibility through calculable risk
- Operates on the ethical foundation of free market exchange

However, in many regions—especially non-Western societies—trust in institutions is often rooted not in mathematics, but in:

- Family structures and community relationships
- Cultural identity and historical memory
- Behavioral traditions and ecological consensus

This has led to the natural exclusivity of current green finance mechanisms: many lands, communities, and regions with authentic ecological value are excluded from global climate finance because they cannot express their value in standardized language.

## 7.2 From Trust Crisis to Systemic Institutional Reconstruction

The differences between civilizations are not just institutional, but emotional and spiritual:

- **Western finance** is fundamentally a numerical-trust system—built on contracts, ratings, insurance, and markets
- **China and most non-Western societies** base trust on relational ethics, social emotion, and deep-rooted cultural consensus

This results in a fundamental conflict: global green finance currently assumes mathematical logic as the only valid mode of expression, excluding all others. Thus, many ecological assets lack access to valuation and funding, not because they are not real, but because they cannot be codified in standard language.

## 7.3 Redefining RWA: Toward a Dual System of Emotion + Evidence

**RWA should be redefined as:** *"Real-world ecological behavior + human social cognition."*

Its verification system must follow a dual-track structure:

| System Dimension | Expression Mechanism | Tool/Model |
|-----------------|---------------------|------------|
| Physical Asset | Satellite, sensors, carbon calculation, AI modeling | MRV + Blockchain Verification + ESG Protocols |
| Social Trust | Community engagement, traditional knowledge, cultural rights, ecological memory | Digital Consensus + Ecological Points Chain + Multilingual Evaluation Models |

Such a system acknowledges that the real world is not only physical—it is also a spiritual and social experiential entity.

We propose an institutional solution: **Multi-Civilizational Ecological Trust Chain (MCETC)**

Finance must trust not only data, but also human ecological actions and cultural continuity.

## 7.4 Financial Legitimacy in the Global South and Climate Valuation Bias

Green finance rating systems are currently dominated by the Global North, with institutions like MSCI, S&P, and CDP leading valuation logic. This causes structural bias:

- Non-English contexts are misunderstood or ignored
- Areas lacking satellite or sensor data are deemed "high-risk"
- Ecological actions in non-market economies are excluded due to lack of ESG-compatible formats

As a result: **The more ecologically authentic a place is, the harder it is to access climate finance.**

This represents systemic injustice—not merely financial exclusion, but civilizational disenfranchisement.

We advocate building an **Ecological Justice Finance Grid (EJFG)**:

| Transition | From → To |
|-----------|-----------|
| Valuation logic | Algorithmic Ratings → Consensus-based Deliberation |
| Trust logic | Data Trust → Cultural Trust + Ecological Behavior |
| Institutional center | Centralized Output → Polycentric Embeddedness |
| Value model | Asset Valuation → Governance Collaboration |

Only when southern, inland, Indigenous, and ecologically fragile areas have asset rights and valuation discourse power, can climate finance be called fair.

## 7.5 Institutional Safeguards and Ethical Mechanisms in Digital Finance

AI's application in finance is expanding rapidly, but so are its hidden risks. We must rebuild institutional logic to ensure that **"AI empowerment ≠ AI domination."**

We must boost efficiency while avoiding:

- Who defines "valid data"? Are remote communities subject to data discrimination?
- Who controls algorithm structures? Is there institutional colonialism at play?
- Who decides if ecological behaviors are fundable? Is cultural diversity being suppressed?

We propose the establishment of a **Green AI Regulatory Protocol (GARP)** with key principles:

- All green finance AI models must undergo transparent registration
- All ecological RWA scoring standards must be subject to public review mechanisms
- All key algorithms must undergo multi-civilizational ethics review

### GARP Protection Principles

| Protection Principle | Content |
|---------------------|---------|
| Data Sovereignty Assurance | Every nation and community controls their own ecological data |
| Algorithm Transparency Audit | All scoring models must pass multi-civilizational ethics review |
| Public Participation Mechanism | All RWA verification systems must allow public questioning and co-decision |
| Marginal Culture Protection | Provide legitimate valuation channels for Indigenous and non-mainstream ecological knowledge |

AI must not become a system of institutional hegemony—it must serve as a supportive expression of human ecological wisdom. As researchers point out, AI's development should draw upon ecological principles, building systems that are more resilient, responsible, and diverse. AI should help identify patterns in complex ecosystems, support sustainable decision-making, and respect Indigenous knowledge.

As **The Oxford Handbook of AI Governance** emphasizes, AI regulation must be rooted in values, forming a multi-level ethical oversight ecosystem.

**In short: AI must become an extension of human ecological wisdom, helping to build a more sustainable, just, and inclusive future.**
