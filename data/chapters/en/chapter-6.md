## 6.1 Spatial System Logic from Global Thermodynamic Mechanisms

In constructing the spatial dimension of climate governance, we must first recognize a fundamental truth:

**The Earth is not a balanced body—it is a dynamic flow structure.**

This means that the development and governance of any region should not be discussed in isolation. We must rebuild an "Upstream–Midstream–Downstream" climate-geographic system map from four interconnected dimensions:

1. Thermal exchange
2. Water-vapor circulation
3. Geological structures
4. Human interventions

The sustainability of any institution is ultimately tested through its practical application in time and space. If the Paris Agreement provided the linguistic system for global climate governance, we now need to construct its geographic prototype—spatial pilot zones that are visible, experiential, and replicable.

### The Imbalance of Global Heat Flow Fields: A Physical Starting Point for Spatial Governance

The Earth's thermal structure presents the following characteristics:

- **Equatorial regions**: Receive the most solar radiation, forming rising air currents and zones of heat accumulation.
- **High-latitude polar regions**: Continuously lose heat, forming low-pressure sinking zones and cold air invasions.
- **Mid-latitude regions**: Act as transition zones where hot and cold meet—home to most of the world's agriculture and urban life.
- **Ocean currents**: Such as the North Atlantic Drift and Equatorial Countercurrent, control global thermal exchange.
- **Topographic buffers**: Plateaus like the Tibetan and Andes influence precipitation and shift flow patterns.
- **Geological rifts and tectonic belts**: Shape sea–land boundaries, geothermal resources, and zones of natural risk.

This forms the foundation of global climate imbalance—a flowing, asymmetric energy field that determines the differentiated destinies of various regions.

### The "Upstream–Downstream" Logic Is Not Linear, But a Nested Functional Network

| Hierarchy | Typical Features | Functional Role | Representative Regions |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Upstream | Plateaus, rainforests, rainfall origins | Zones of heat and moisture generation | Tibetan Plateau, Amazon, Congo Basin |
| Midstream | River plains, agricultural belts, second-tier cities | Energy transformation and social production zones | Southeast Asia rice regions, Delhi Basin |
| Downstream | Ports, deltas, urban clusters, desert fringes | Output, convergence, degradation, recycling zones | Bangkok, Dakar, Rotterdam, Gulf States |

Thus, governance must build a responsibility chain from the source, including:
- Redirecting eco-protective finance toward upstream regions
- Focusing infrastructure investment in midstream areas
- Deploying adaptive governance and energy transition in downstream regions
- Establishing a "Climate Responsibility Agreement Network" among upstream, midstream, and downstream actors—not just a carbon market

### Integration of Arid–Cold Zones, Coastal Systems, and Civilizational Rebuilding Mechanisms

Within the upstream–midstream–downstream logic:

- **Arid–cold zones** (e.g., HANBANG belt, Central Asia, Siberia) lie at critical convergence points, combining ecological reserves with transformation pressures—they are the "pivot axis of global climate mechanisms."
- **Coastal systems** (e.g., Southern Europe, East Asia, West Africa) are the most fragile downstream pressure points, yet also serve as windows for institutional and technological export.
- The **mountain–basin–coast continuum** forms the spatial backbone for institutional evolution, which should be integrated as a "Unified Climate Institutional Corridor."

#### The HANBANG Zone—Spanning the Mongolian Plateau, Central Asia, southern Siberia, and northern China

This zone possesses four key traits:

1. **Ecologically fragile but high potential**: Vast lands, latent water, abundant wind
2. **Politically peripheral but stable**: Low geopolitical tension, suitable for international cooperation
3. **Sparsely populated but culturally diverse**: Holds potential for civilizational bridging
4. **Significantly impacted by climate change**: Located at the frontier of reforestation, desertification, and drying river systems

We propose establishing this area as a **"Cold Belt Ecological Governance Experimental Corridor"** jointly developed by China and the EU, focusing on:

- Distributed clean energy systems (wind–solar–hydrogen)
- AI-driven water–soil monitoring networks + climate disaster warning systems
- Transboundary ecological restoration corridors (grasslands–forests–rivers)
- Climate refugee settlement and digital eco-employment support

#### Coastal Transition Corridors: The Frontline of Governance Boundaries

Coasts are not only economic hubs—they are the frontlines of climate change. Regions such as the Mediterranean, Red Sea, Southeast Asia, and China's Yangtze and Pearl River Deltas face several systemic challenges:

- Urban over-concentration and heat island effects
- Sea-level rise and saltwater intrusion
- High carbon-dependence of port industries and transition dilemmas
- Aging infrastructure and insufficient climate adaptability

We propose the creation of **"Smart Climate Bay Pilot Zones"** featuring China–EU joint ecological infrastructure systems:

- Smart port management systems (AI-based emissions scheduling + green shipping)
- Coastal greenbelt construction and urban coastal renewal
- Integration of blue carbon mechanisms (e.g., mangroves, seagrass) into the RWA system
- Establishment of "Climate Complementarity Regional Cooperation Mechanisms" between coasts and inland cold–dry belts (e.g., resource for data, carbon rights for eco-products)

## 6.2 Global Spatial Governance Networks and Institutional Interface Mechanisms

If Section 6.1 clarified that global climate governance must begin with the thermodynamic and flow-based structure of the Earth's physical systems, then the next step is to consider:

- Why is there a major mismatch between the spatial layout of human cities/infrastructure and natural flow systems?
- How can we reconcile the disjunction between spatial distribution and climate mechanisms through institutional logic?

### Historical Inertia of Urban Layouts and Resource-Driven Logic

The current spatial distribution of global urban systems has been heavily shaped by historical mechanisms:

- **Resource-driven orientation**: Coal mines, ports, and plains with water networks determined the original distribution of industrial cities.
- **Trade-driven orientation**: Estuaries, shipping routes, and isthmuses were prioritized, forming "coastal advantage urban clusters."
- **Colonial spatial logic**: Cities were embedded as control nodes within external resource systems, rather than evolving from endogenous ecological structures.

These layouts maximized the extraction of natural resources but failed to establish energy balance mechanisms with nature. For example:

- Industrial cities ignored prevailing wind directions, worsening the urban heat island effect
- Estuary cities compressed flood retention zones, negatively affecting watershed climates
- Cities at the foot of mountains created microclimate heat-trap zones, intensifying dryness and heat

As a result, carbon emission distributions are seriously misaligned with natural wind–water–heat corridors, making governance far more difficult.

### Spatial Governance Logic Should Be Based on Natural Systems

We propose a new logic of spatial governance, grounded in the principle:

**"Let natural systems be the skeleton, infrastructure be the nervous system, and institutions be the consciousness"**

This rebuilds the layout and operation of human spatial systems. The structure is as follows:

| Dimension | Goal | Implementation Path |
|-----------|------|---------------------|
| Natural System Mapping | Identify global wind corridors, water systems, and heat transport routes | Construct a global "ecological functional flow map" |
| Spatial Adaptation Deployment | Reassess urban locations, industrial layout, and population density | Launch a "spatial rebalancing policy package" |
| Institutional Interface Mechanism | Establish a governance protocol between cities and natural systems | Formulate Spatial–Climate Response Protocols (SCRP) |

At its core, cities and infrastructure of the future will no longer burden natural systems, but instead serve as collaborative nodes in climate-ecological regulation networks.

### Nodalization of the Global Spatial Network: Toward a Multipolar Spatial Order

At the institutional level, we should promote the construction of a **Global Spatial Governance Grid (GSGG)**—a network composed of:

**1. Climate Nexus Cities**

- Located at wind corridor intersections and moisture transformation zones
- Responsible for climate regulation and policy coordination
- Examples: Ürümqi, Cairo, Brasília, Windhoek, Prague

**2. Eco-Computational Nodes**

- Based in ecologically sensitive or restorative regions
- Aggregate capabilities such as remote sensing, AI modeling, carbon accounting
- Examples: Shannan (Tibet), Eastern Gobi (Mongolia), Central African Highlands

**3. Institutional Docking Ports**

- Located at coastal and estuarine zones
- Pilot mechanisms for data, carbon credits, and ecosystem service exchanges
- Examples: Shekou (Shenzhen), Port of Rotterdam, Marseille, Zanzibar

These nodes will be organically connected to form a three-dimensional global climate governance network integrating institutions, technology, and ecology.

### Spatial Rebalancing Is Not About Relocation, But Institutional Redesign

We do not advocate for catastrophic solutions such as "destroying cities and relocating populations." Instead, we emphasize:

- Within existing spatial patterns, use technology + finance + policy to achieve load redistribution and functional reconfiguration
- Use AI-based digital twins of cities to simulate wind–heat–water pathways, optimizing green space allocation and restoring wind corridors
- Promote a restructured spatial value system, allowing non-core cities to share in global governance benefits through ecological credits and carbon sink assets

**Humanity is not the master of space—we are but one link in the climate chain.**

Our task is not to rebuild cities, but to rebuild the relational structure between cities and nature.

## 6.3 The Significance of Spatial Prototypes: From Points to Corridors, From Corridors to Rings

Why deploy institutional experiments geographically? Because space is the most realistic testing ground for ecological systems. Institutions only reveal their justice, efficiency, and replicability through operation across time and space.

We propose the **Geo-Institutional Prototyping Methodology (GIPM)** with the following logic:

| Scale | Core Goal | Application Tools |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| Point (City or Node) | Create institutional models, rapidly perceptible | ESG pilot zones, micro-scale policy sandboxes |
| Corridor (Regional Belt) | Develop multi-element collaborative governance | Distributed energy + AI networks + governance platforms |
| Ring (Cross-Regional Network) | Expand international collaboration and data mutual recognition | Joint carbon markets, RWA information exchange platforms |
