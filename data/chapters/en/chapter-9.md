No climate governance system can rely solely on government efforts, nor be dominated entirely by corporations.

The essence of climate leadership is a transgenerational mechanism of value transmission, which requires long-term commitment, public spirit, and moral responsibility. Compared to this, governments are constrained by political cycles, and businesses by profit motives. Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs), however, serve as vital carriers of public ethics and institutional continuity.

Especially in the realm of climate and ecology, NGOs possess:
- **Interdisciplinary integration capabilities**: Linking research, policy, and social action
- **Institutional oversight capacity**: Providing checks on both public and private sectors
- **Social mobilization skills**: Stimulating public participation and building community consensus
- **Moral legitimacy**: Commanding global influence in shaping public agendas

## 9.1 NGOs Are Not Marginal Players, but Central Organizers in Governance Structures

Traditionally, NGOs have been treated as auxiliary actors or external advocates. However, in modern climate governance, NGOs should serve as central nodes in platform-based governance structures, undertaking three primary roles:

**Value Mediator**: Establishing moral dialogue platforms between governments, businesses, and the public

**Agenda Designer**: Framing key issues, setting priorities, and guiding public budget allocations

**Consensus Incubator**: Fostering grassroots consensus networks and multilateral dialogue through action-based engagement

NGOs hold a natural advantage in early intervention, long-term commitment, and cross-regional coordination—especially in areas like cold-region governance, arid-region revitalization, and carbon-neutral pilot zones.

## 9.2 NGO + Enterprise Collaborative Governance: Dual Structure, Multi-Path Model

Businesses are not to be excluded—they should act as co-executors under the leadership of NGOs. We propose a **"Value-Oriented Governance Alliance"** model, structured as follows:

| Role | Function | Examples |
|------|----------|----------|
| NGO | Set agendas, define rules, ensure transparency | Friends of the Earth, NRDC |
| Enterprise | Execute projects, provide resources, respond to markets | Renewable energy firms, digital tech firms |
| Public Platform | Build collaboration frameworks, record feedback and credit | ESG platforms, green finance centres |

NGOs aim to maximize ecological value, not profit. In turn, they steer business behaviour toward systemic justice. Enterprises benefit by gaining public trust, policy connections, and societal resources—a mutually reinforcing structure.

## 9.3 Governance for Citizens (2C): Structuring Social Justice and Behavioural Guidance

The ultimate goal of governance is not to punish corporations or individuals but to build a public environment that systemically guides behaviour and reinforces shared values. This requires three interconnected systems:

**Transparency System** – Right to know: ESG ratings, environmental risk alerts, policy interpretation

**Incentive System** – Right to participate: carbon points, green rewards, community credit platforms

**Feedback System** – Right to express: complaint mechanisms, digital democracy tools, data participation

For example, in a **"Green Township Initiative,"** residents can record low-carbon behaviours via a smart app, earning points redeemable for benefits. Once companies disclose their actions transparently, consumers can support more sustainable brands—forming a closed loop of behaviour–structure–consciousness.

## 9.4 A Consensus-Based Institutional Framework with NGOs at the Core

We propose a **"Three-Dimensional Social Collaboration Mechanism"** centred on NGOs:

- **Vertical Axis (Value Coordination)**: NGO + Government → Balance between institutional legitimacy and public ethics
- **Horizontal Axis (Action Organization)**: NGO + Enterprise → Integration of project execution capabilities
- **Bottom-Up Axis (Community Consensus)**: NGO + Civil Society → Awareness cultivation and empowerment

This framework does not replace governments or markets. Rather, it acts as a **"governance harmonizer"** in the climate era, guiding society toward a more value-driven, intelligent, and ethical form.

## 9.5 Case Study: Kunlun Ecological Protection Association

The Kunlun Ecological Protection Association is more than a guardian of nature—it is a model for future ecological governance, offering a Chinese solution of **"Technology + Co-Governance"** for global arid regions.

Founded within the ESG and climate leadership framework, this regional NGO rejects the traditional development-first model. Instead, it starts with ecological restoration as the primary industry, utilizing:
- High-mountain snowmelt water supply systems
- Saline-alkali land remediation
- Low-excavation landform engineering

Simultaneously, it integrates AI remote sensing, digital carbon assets, and ESG evaluation systems, transforming barren lands into green growth zones with both ecological value and sustainable industry potential.

Through DAO governance mechanisms and ecological sensing platforms, it connects science, engineering, and community participation to advance:
- Mountain water source protection
- Salt-affected land remediation
- Green, low-carbon development

NGOs become the collaborative centre of ecological governance. On this foundation, the association is building a **"Climate Bank Model"** combining NGOs and green foundations. Using carbon rights pledging, ecological credits, and philanthropic capital, it provides financing loops and risk safeguards for restoration projects.

This model merges ESG credit assessment with community co-governance, treating ecosystems as operational value units, guiding arid regions worldwide toward a **"finance-embedded ecological governance"** paradigm.
