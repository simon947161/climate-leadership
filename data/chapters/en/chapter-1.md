# Chapter 1: Introduction | The Judgment of the Future

We are entering a historical juncture in which the essence of climate leadership is being radically redefined. This is no longer merely a question of reducing emissions within a given year, nor is it limited to setting timetables for carbon peaking. Rather, it is a question of profound judgment regarding the trajectory of human development. This capacity for judgment will determine whether, within the critical window of global order transition, we can truly guide the emergence of a new ecological civilization order.

If we observe with sufficient clarity, we will recognize that we have already entered an era in which human society, technological systems, and the natural environment are thoroughly intertwined. This is not a question of whether growth will continue, but rather a question of how development itself must be redefined. And climate change is precisely the frontline of this redefinition.

We might understand future development as a vast mechanism of resource reallocation. This allocation no longer depends on traditional territory, capital, or military power. Instead, it hinges on who can anticipate the future earlier, and who can earlier organize systems that align with the future order. In this sense, so-called "strategy" is not merely a matter of policy design; it is a civilizational community's prophetic感知 (perception) of the essence of the future world order and its capacity for systematic construction.

We argue that this judgment must respond to the following precondition:

- **Deep Reunderstanding of the Climate System**: Our understanding of ecological vulnerable zones—cold zones, arid zones, coastlines—must shift from disaster narratives to systemic planning.
- **Restructuring Zonal Responsibility**: The responsibility mechanisms between Global South and North, developing and developed nations, resource zones and emission zones must be reorganized.
- **Systematizing Distributed Governance Infrastructure**: This is not merely digital transformation, but a structural redesign of ecological and social governance.
- **New Social Energy Mechanisms under AI Dominance**: AI is not merely a tool, but an institutional catalyst.
- **The Civilizational Character and Ethical Bottom Line of Institutions**: What ultimately determines whether a system can be accepted by humanity long-term is whether it is fair, temperate, and visionary.

## Introduction: Between Information Overload and Judgment Scarcity, Who Will Coordinate the Future?

We are situated in an era of "information overload and judgment scarcity." Whether it is the 24-hour news cycle, AI modeling, emergency policies, or the endless stream of green finance and energy technologies, everything is updating at high speed, yet failing to form a clear future direction. What we possess is fragmented response, lacking systematic coordination.

Confronting increasingly frequent climate disasters and geopolitical gamesmanship, no single government or market can independently respond to the holistic variation of the Earth's ecosystem. In this context, "climate leadership" has been placed on the global agenda. But we must be vigilant: true leadership is not merely the numerical representation of declining carbon emission curves, nor is it solely the complex combination of institutions and market tools. It is a comprehensive test of the depth of human spirit and the capacity for future judgment.

And the construction of this judgment cannot be completed by a single knowledge system or unilateral national will. This is precisely the critical space where Chinese and European civilizations can jointly unfold.

**China**, possessing vast ecological foundations, complex geographical structures, and powerful systemic execution capacity, is the real driving field of the global green transition.

**Europe**, possessing profound institutional design experience, philosophical traditions, and public value frameworks, is the experimental field of global climate institutional innovation.

When these two civilizational logics truly converge—no longer stopping at the technical docking of cooperation frameworks, but jointly facing the fundamental proposition of "how to understand the future"—can a genuine "joint climate leadership" emerge.

This is also the original intention behind the birth of the "Thermal-Time-Environment Governance (TTEG)" theory. We recognize that the core of the climate problem lies not only in "emission volume," but in the imbalance of "rhythm of thermal disturbance" and "response time of ecosystems." From urban nighttime heat island effects to the alteration of regional water vapor pathways, the "thermal-temporal" distribution of human behavior is reconstructing natural rhythms.

The TTEG theory attempts to re-plan the governance logic of cities, industries, and ecological boundaries with heat flow, temporal rhythms, and feedback mechanisms at its core. This is a product of the integration of Western institutional design and Eastern temporal philosophy, and it is also the starting point of dynamic ecological governance in the AI era.

## The "2-8-70 Civilization Architecture"

Against this backdrop, we propose a new cognitive model for global structure: the **"2-8-70 Civilization Architecture."**

| Number | Meaning |
|--------|----------|
| **2** | China and Europe as dual-core civilization bodies, possessing the leading capacity to integrate the future agenda in cognitive structure, governance systems, and public responsibility. |
| **8** | The existing institutional sovereignty core of the globe (G7 + China and India) constitutes an institutional cluster for rule output, serving as the design hub for future mechanism construction. |
| **70** | The extensive zones where the remaining global population resides—this is both the frontline of climate shock and the area with the weakest governance capacity on the eve of interstellar civilization transition. |

What we must face is a civilizational divide far deeper than the "North-South divide": without a symbiotic order driven by the 2, with the 8 serving the 70, this planet will be unable to welcome the prelude of interstellar civilization.

And the key to constructing this pattern is not a certain power output, nor is it the institutional exclusion of a new Cold War style. Rather, it is the reconstruction of a shared judgment structure in the AI era. We no longer debate "who leads," but must jointly answer:

> "Do we truly understand the future?" —A future of climate, humanity, technology, spirit, and institutions.

This is precisely the original intention of this book. We believe that the deep integration of Chinese and European civilizations, the institutional translation capacity of TTEG theory, and the co-governance mechanism constructed for 70% of the global population can provide a genuine, feasible, and shared spiritual framework for the coming new climate era and the community with a shared future for mankind.
