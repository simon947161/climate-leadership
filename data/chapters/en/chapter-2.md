We are entering a new era in which climatic zonal sovereignty must be redefined.

In the past, we were accustomed to classifying development stages based on national boundaries, GDP, per capita income, and degree of industrialization. These metrics were then used to determine each country's position and obligations within climate governance. However, the real climate system does not follow national borders. It operates according to units defined by geographical latitude, topography and hydrology, ecological pressure, and population carrying capacity.

Therefore, in constructing future climate leadership and cooperation mechanisms, we must introduce a new cognitive model: the **Climatic-Zonal Responsibility Mechanism (CZRM)**.

## 2.1 Climate Zones as Foundations for Institutional Negotiation

We can preliminarily categorize the world into four climate zone responsibility models:

**High Disaster Frequency Zones** (extreme heat, drought, typhoons)
→ Require greater resource and technology transfers; hold adaptation priority rights.

**High Emissions Concentration Zones** (densely industrialized regions)
→ Must bear obligations for technological spillover and climate investment responsibility.

**Carbon Sink Potential Zones** (forests, plateaus, wetlands)
→ Require institutional protection and green finance, holding ecological service rights.

**Resource Transition Zones** (former fossil-fuel-based energy economies)
→ Need support for energy substitution, holding green revival rights.

This mechanism is not about punishing or ranking countries, but about creating a new consensus framework for reordering global climate responsibilities.

## 2.2 Cold and Arid Zones: From Blind Spots to the Core of Global Governance

In current climate governance discourse, northern cold zones and global arid zones—such as the Horn of Africa, Central Asia, the Arabian Peninsula, Northwest China, and inland Australia—are often marginalized due to limited data, weak representation, and low participation.

Yet these zones are precisely the "transitional belts" and "experimental grounds" of future climate civilization:

- Cold zones will become new havens for water sources, agriculture, and biodiversity in a warming world;
- Arid zones will become the main battlegrounds for ecological restoration, energy transition, and smart governance.

Therefore, these zones must be given priority in China–Europe climate governance collaboration, with enhanced institutional participation rights, data sovereignty, and project incubation privileges.

## 2.3 Establishing a Cognitive Model for "Carbon Development Rights"

We propose promoting a new concept within global climate governance: the **Carbon Development Right (CDR)**. This is not a repackaging of carbon quotas, but a recognition that every ecological region:

- Possesses irreplaceable value in ecological service;
- Has a historically legitimate development path;
- Deserves institutional negotiation and participatory rights in governance.

From this perspective, future carbon markets and green credit systems should no longer be centered solely on emissions, but evaluated based on "ecological responsibility, governance execution, and public participation."

## 2.4 Reconstructing the China–Europe Cooperation Platform with "Zonal Responsibility"

In the complex architecture of global climate governance, the nation-state is no longer the sole effective unit of action. With the emergence of TTEG theory and ecological chain logic, we propose using "climate responsibility zones" as new institutional spatial units—transcending national borders and restructuring the China–Europe cooperation framework based on ecological functions and environmental vulnerability.

We propose establishing a **China–Europe Climate Co-Governance Pilot Zone** platform, with each pilot zone built around a typical climate zone:

- **Arctic fringe zones**: Joint monitoring of heat diffusion pathways and ice cap feedback mechanisms;
- **Arid inland regions**: Collaborating on AI-powered irrigation, atmospheric moisture compensation, and desertification control projects;
- **Coastal impact zones**: Joint response to storm surges, heatwaves, and extreme humid-heat weather;
- **Plateau and mountain ecosystems**: Co-developing alpine carbon sink mechanisms and thermal disturbance time modulation models.

Each responsibility zone will serve as an experimental platform for institutional geography, embedding four types of governance innovation pathways:

1. **Dynamic ESG Response System**: Real-time integration of heat-time-carbon data to reflect multidimensional ecological performance.
2. **RWA Ecological Asset Evaluation System**: Converting land, water, and ecological behaviors into tradable carbon/heat/water values.
3. **Collaborative Governance Experimentation**: Creating dual AI–human consensus frameworks to test integration of DAO structures with local consultation mechanisms.
4. **Ecological Contribution Feedback System**: Designing community-based benefit-sharing mechanisms to reflect the value of ecological behavior.

Through this approach, China–Europe cooperation will transcend abstract policy dialogue and become a measurable, verifiable, and replicable institutional synergy model—one that not only deepens bilateral ties but also provides a governance template for the 70% of the world's population living in ecologically vulnerable regions. This is a true institutional prototype reconstruction in service of a shared human future.
